Basic Conditions for this Section 📋

  • The population distribution is normal.
  • The population standard deviation \(\sigma\) is known.
  • The sampling distribution of \(\overline X\sim N(\mu, \sigma^2/n)\)

🎮 Video Game Reaction Times

💡 A video game developer claims the average reaction time to dodge an obstacle is \(\mu = 300\) ms with known standard deviation \(\sigma = 40\) ms.
You collect a random sample of \(n = 25\) players and find a sample mean of \(\bar{x} = 312\) ms.

Assume \(\mu\) is unknown to us and we wish to use \(\bar{x}\) to estimate it.

We start with the probability statement: \[ P\left(-z_{\alpha/2} < \frac{\overline{X} - \mu}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}} < z_{\alpha/2}\right) = 1 - \alpha \]

Tip

\(z_{\alpha/2}\) is the critical value from the standard normal distribution such that the middle \(1 - \alpha\) of the distribution lies between \(-z_{\alpha/2}\) and \(z_{\alpha/2}\).

The 95% Confidence Interval Idea 🎯

  • Suppose we observe data \(X_1, X_2, \dots, X_n\).
  • The sample mean \(\bar{x}\) is used to estimate the population mean \(\mu\).
  • We create an interval around \(\bar{x}\) that is likely to contain \(\mu\).

\[ \bar{x} \pm 1.96\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} \]

General \((1 - \alpha)100\%\) Confidence Interval 💡

  • For any confidence level \((1 - \alpha) \cdot 100\%\), the CI is:

\[ \left(\bar{x} - z_{\alpha/2}\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}, \bar{x} + z_{\alpha/2}\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}\right) \]

Tip

You can also write it as: \(\bar{x} \pm z_{\alpha/2}\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}\)

Pivotal Quantities 🔁

Let \(X_1, X_2, \dots, X_n\) be the sample.

A pivotal quantity is a random variable that:

  1. Depends on the data and the parameter \(\theta\).
  2. Has a probability distribution that does not depend on the value of \(\theta\).

We use the known distribution of the pivotal quantity to construct the interval.

Note

This method of constructing confidence intervals is called the pivotal quantity method.

Calculating the Confidence Interval 🎮

  • From our video game example:
    • \(\bar{x} = 312\) ms
    • \(\sigma = 40\) ms
    • \(n = 25\)
    • \(z_{0.025} = 1.96\) for 95% confidence

\[ 312 \pm 1.96 \cdot \frac{40}{\sqrt{25}} = 312 \pm 15.68 \]

Note

So our 95% confidence interval for the true average reaction time is: \[ (296.32, 327.68) \text{ ms} \] so the claim that \(\mu=300\) is plausible for our population.

Margin of Error and Sample Size 🔢

  • The margin of error is: \(ME=z_{\alpha/2}\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}\)
  • To ensure the margin of error is at most \(ME\), solve for \(n\):

\[ n = \left(\frac{z_{\alpha/2} \cdot \sigma}{ME}\right)^2 \]

Designing for Precision 🎮📏

  • The game developers want to estimate average reaction time more precisely.
  • They’d like the margin of error to be no more than \(ME = 5\) ms with 95% confidence.
  • Recall:
    • \(\sigma = 40\) ms
    • \(z_{0.025} = 1.96\)

What sample size \(n\) is needed?

We use the formula from the previous slide to find \(n\):

\[ n = \left(\frac{z_{\alpha/2} \cdot \sigma}{ME}\right)^2 = \left(\frac{1.96 \cdot 40}{5}\right)^2 = 245.86 \]

Tip

✅ So we need at least 246 players in the sample to achieve a 95% CI with a margin of error no greater than 5 milliseconds.

Summary 📌

  • Confidence intervals provide a range of plausible values for \(\mu\)
  • Normality and known \(\sigma\) simplify calculations
  • Use \(z_{\alpha/2}\) based on desired confidence level
  • The required margin of error helps guide sample size decisions
  • Pivotal quantities are key tools for general CI construction
